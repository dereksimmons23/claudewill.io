The Call

You got the call. Position eliminated. Thirty years of work reduced to a meeting that lasted less than an hour.

Or maybe it wasn't a call. Maybe it was a slow fade. The title got smaller. The seat at the table moved farther from the center. The org chart changed and your name wasn't on it.

Either way, you're here now. Fifty-something. Decades of expertise. A professional identity that just ended. And a world full of advice from people who haven't been where you are.

I have.

The Fork

Everyone who gets pushed out at 50 stares at the same three paths. I stared at all three.

Get Hired Again

Apply to jobs designed for people 15 years younger. Translate 30 years of experience into keywords an algorithm can parse. Compete on salary against people with lower mortgages. Watch your inbox fill with silence. 64% of workers over 50 report age discrimination. The average 55-64 year old spends 27 weeks unemployed — four times longer than younger workers.

Consult

Trade your expertise for hourly rates. Build a slide deck. Network. Pitch. Chase invoices. It works — if you can land the first client. And the second. And if you can build a system that doesn't depend on you being in the room for every conversation.

Build

Take everything you know and build a practice around it. Not a job. Not a gig. A system that works when you're not watching. That serves clients from anywhere. That runs on your terms, not someone else's org chart. This is the hardest path. It's also the one where you own what you build.

I chose all three. Applied to jobs. Landed consulting work. And built the system underneath all of it. The system is what made the rest work.

What I Built

October 2024, my position was eliminated after 30 years in media. First Chief Creative Officer at a top-10 US newspaper. Generated $20 million during a pandemic. None of it mattered when the spreadsheet changed.

In 12 months, I built an AI-native consulting practice from scratch. Not by reading about AI. By building with it — every day, every session, every project.

Here's what the system actually looks like:

One command, ten projects
cw [project] — switches context instantly. Client work, product builds, personal projects, writing. Each one has its own identity file, its own state, its own session history. One person, one CLI, the whole board.
Sessions that remember
Every session starts warm. The system loads the last three session memories and the current project state automatically. No re-explaining. No "where were we?" Every session ends by writing what happened to a database. The next one picks up where this one left off.
Morning standup, evening handoff
Same discipline I used leading 55-person newsroom teams. Show up, read the board, pick the work, do the work, hand off clean. The material changed. The work ethic didn't.
Reusable workflows
Meeting processing. Deliverable tracking. Content distribution. Project scaffolding. Health checks. End-of-day documentation. Not scripts on a shelf — rituals that run every day across every project.

The Method

Everything runs on four steps. They work for a morning session, a client engagement, or a career rebuild.

Start

Know where you are. Read the board. What's active? What's stale? What needs attention today? Don't start working until you know the landscape.

Work

Do the work. Not the planning about the work. Not the meeting about the work. The actual work. Build, ship, iterate. Call and response.

Finish

Leave it clean. Document what happened. Hand off state so the next session — or the next person — can pick up without re-explaining. The handoff is part of the work.

Decide

Look from multiple angles. Business lens. Quality lens. Connection lens. Pushback lens. Don't decide from one angle when the decision affects everything.
