LIGHTNING/bug

Two kinds of light. ~7 minutes. Built from terminal.

Derek Claude Simmons — March 2025 / March 2026

Director's Statement

I grew up in a single-wide on North Nickerson Street in Kansas. Aluminum. When it rained it was like the end of days. My mom and my sister raised me there — Shannon was twelve when I was eight and she started the job early. We had a wooden porch where my mom made sun tea by day and we used it for stargazing at night. Someone stole it while we were at K-Mart.

Back then there were lightning bugs in the summer. Thick. They'd light up the whole sky. I wrote a short film about a boy and a bug in March 2025. Didn't know what it meant yet. A year later, at a bark park in Minnesota, the boy in the wheat field started talking. He told the man to get out of the chair. If you need two words, you know what they are.

I built the whole thing from terminal. No editing software. An AI cloned my voice and I rejected it. The machines dreamed the images. My real voice sings the song at the end. My mom's real voicemail opens the film — her calling "Marco," waiting for an answer. The film is the answer.

The film got quieter. Less narration. Sound design carries it now — rain on tin, a transformer hum, crickets, the purr of a lightning bug. Then a song called "Until It Ain't" breaks through at the end. I sing it. That part is real.
