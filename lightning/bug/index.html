lightning/bug

The absence of darkness. ~8 minutes. Built from terminal.

Derek Claude Simmons — March 2025 / March 2026

Director's Statement

I grew up in a single-wide on North Nickerson Street in Kansas. Aluminum. When it rained it was like the end of days. My mom and my sister raised me there — Shannon was twelve when I was eight and she started the job early. We had a wooden porch where my mom made sun tea by day and we used it for stargazing at night. Someone stole it while we were at K-Mart.

Back then there were lightning bugs in the summer. Thick. They'd light up the whole sky. The mean kids would knock them down with wiffleball bats to make their bats glow. I wrote a short film about a boy and a bug in March 2025. Didn't know what it meant yet. A year later, at a bark park in Minnesota, the boy in the wheat field started talking. He told the man to get out of the chair. If you need two words, you know what they are.

I built the whole thing from terminal. No editing software. An AI cloned my voice and I rejected it. The machines dreamed the images. My real voice sings the song at the end. My mom's real voicemail opens the film — her calling "Marco," waiting for an answer. The film is the answer.

The visual register is built from what I see when I close my eyes and think about that wheat field. No named artists. No borrowed aesthetics. No anime. I spent a night reviewing every still we had — fifty-six images I'd saved over months, twenty-three storyboard frames, real photographs of my eye, my mother, the actual wiffle ball bat. From that I built a stylebook: photoreal but painterly, darkness IS the style, two kinds of light that never mix (cold cobalt for the bolt, warm amber for the bug), silhouettes over rendered faces. The darker a frame is, the more graphic it becomes. When there's less light, there's less detail to expose — and the image becomes pure shape. I built the style from what the machine found when I described what I remembered. That's the only honest version.

The narration is thirty-one minutes of unscripted iPhone recordings — chopped, not scripted. The voice is the first take. I kept the contradictions. Sound design carries the rest: a storm system on aluminum, a transformer dying, crickets on the path home, the purr of a lightning bug landing on a bat, a ballpark organ as a boy walks to the plate, a thunder crack when he makes contact. Then a song called "Here We Are, Kid" breaks through at the end. I wrote it. My voice carries it. The performance is a collaboration with a machine I gave thirty seconds of my voice to. That part is real too.

Every shot in this film is locked. The camera does not move. This was a decision, not a default. Memories don't have camera operators. When you close your eyes and remember a wheat field at night, the field doesn't dolly. It holds. The light moves — the fireflies drift, the wheat bends, the glow travels down the bat — but the frame stays still, the way a memory stays still. The camera language in this film is expressed through illumination, not lens movement. The audience doesn't need the camera to move. They need to breathe.

The model is the lightning. The prompt is the bug.

Production Log

Day 7 (Apr 14) Narration locked. All 33 clips reviewed. Ending confirmed: one whispered line — "I just wanted to see it too" — then black, then the song. Sound design locked: storm system built from layers (rain on aluminum, golf-ball hail, lightning, howling wind), ballpark organ downloaded for the walk to the plate, thunder crack for contact, kitten purr for Shannon landing on the bat. The locked camera named as intentional. Five days to deadline.
Day 6 (Apr 13) Anime out. Built the Derek Claude Simmons stylebook from scratch — reviewed fifty-six saved stills and twenty-three storyboard frames, yes/no/correction on every one. Core rules: photoreal but painterly, darkness is the style, two kinds of light (cold cobalt bolt / warm amber firefly), plus a third (green fluorescent hospital — the in-between). Generated two new locked stills: a firefly landing on a yellow wiffle ball bat and the moment the transformer bolt hits the field and the amber starts going dark. Song is locked. Character references locked. Six days to go.
Day 5 (Apr 12) Pivot day. Every still rebuilt in isekai anime register — ethical call, not aesthetic. Photoreal synthesis of real people was starting to feel like false evidence. 23-shot spine held. A 39-minute sequence walk at the bark park, transcribed by MLX Whisper. The boy in the wheat field told the man to get out of the chair. Score bed fix: the instrumental now fades out before the full song lands, killing a phase-doubling artifact at 3:41. Narration moves from monologue to six whispered phrases. Nano Banana 2 emerged as the character anchor — faces, freckles, the N trucker cap all consistent where FLUX wasn't. Seven days to go.
Day 4 (Apr 11) Rebuild day. Ken Burns on stills was why the cut looked like a slideshow — every shot gets motion now, directed, no crossfades. Runway AIF delivery spec verified: nine days, one shot. Thirteen motion prompts drafted. The song hit its pocket in Suno at 11:19 PM and changed its own name along the way. Pretty sins, kin.
Day 3 (Apr 10) Song started. Three whispered lines recorded on a walk with the dog. Five new stills generated. Forty-five minutes of voice notes transcribed by Whisper. The film knows what it is now.
Day 2 (Apr 9) The film found its spine. Shot list rewritten from scratch. Submission materials drafted. Eight shower vocal takes extracted and cataloged. The boy in the wheat field started talking.
Day 1 (Apr 8) Full asset inventory. Research on nine festivals. Four overnight agents: shot list, sound design, submission materials, AI filmmaking research. Twenty-three documents in the repo by morning.
Day 0 (Apr 3) The film changed. Less narration. More quiet. Sound design carries it. A song breaks through at the end. The boy talks to the man. The man gets one line.
