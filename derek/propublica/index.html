Project 1

Denied Justice

Investigative Series — Star Tribune, 2018

In 2018, the Star Tribune published "Denied Justice" — a nine-part investigation into how Minnesota law enforcement systematically failed sexual assault survivors. More than 1,000 cases examined. 74% never sent to prosecutors. Rapists left free to assault again.

I was CCO and VP. I'm credited on design alongside Anna Boone and Josh Penrod. But my role wasn't to design it. My role was to guide it, art direct it, and build the process that produced that type of work.

The reporters had the story and the data. The newsroom didn't want to commit the resources — a special section, a dedicated digital presentation, the photography and graphics investment. I made the case for why this investigation deserved the visual treatment. Then I did what I'd been doing for years: someone brings me something, we talk through it, they go try something, we iterate. The typography-only cover, the portrait approach, the restraint — those came out of that process. Renée Jones Schneider's photography gave the survivors dignity the system had denied them. Not all of the decisions were mine. But the process was.

If I could do it differently, I wish I hadn't had to fight so hard to do the obvious right thing. A nine-part investigation into sexual assault deserved the visual commitment from the start. The fact that it took convincing is something I think about.

The series was a Pulitzer Prize finalist and won the SPJ Public Service award. Minnesota changed four laws. But the real proof is what happened next. The process became the template. When George Floyd was murdered in our city, the system we'd built produced the coverage that won the 2021 Pulitzer for Breaking News. I was on the business side by then. I wasn't in the room. I didn't need to be.

That's what I mean by visual editing. It's not about me. It's about the work.

Project 2

Prince: Remembering a Minnesota Icon

Breaking News / Commemorative — Star Tribune, 2016

Scroll → Breaking news front page, four zoned covers, and selected interior pages.

Prince died on Thursday, April 21, 2016. By Friday morning we published "Purple Majesty" — the breaking news front page. Digital coverage went up immediately. Over the next 10 days, I art directed a 16-page commemorative section with four unique zoned covers for different regions of our Sunday audience, with a dedicated digital presentation that brought the full section online.

I commissioned Robert Carter to paint two original cover illustrations, completed in less than a week. One cover used a staff photograph. For the fourth, I negotiated the rights to a rarely seen 1977 photograph by Robert Whitman, taken when Prince was 19 — before his first record deal. Only 15 press kits from those shoots were ever produced. Jon Bream, the longest-running music critic in America, wrote the essays. The section drew from 40 years of Star Tribune coverage.

Every paper in the country was running Prince coverage. The editorial decisions across those 10 days is the backstory. When to use illustration vs. photography. How to hold the tone of a state's mourning without exploiting it. How to give four audiences four different visual interpretations of one enormous loss.

I color corrected the images, approved the final proofs, and was at Heritage, our printing facility, when they loaded the sections into the delivery trucks. The relationship with Carter wasn't new — I'd been commissioning him since 2008 at the LA Times. That existing network is the only reason two original paintings were possible in less than a week.

At the LA Times, I worked on the John Wooden commemorative section that was distributed at Pauley Pavilion for his memorial service. The night Michael Jackson died, I was the only design director in the building — working with an intern to produce the next-day coverage. I brought that process to the Star Tribune.

Prince reprints still sell today — 10 years later. The commemorative section became the proof of concept for Star Tribune Magazine, which launched the following year with Prince on the debut cover.

Project 3

claudewill.io

Platform — Self-Initiated, 2024–2026

In October 2024, my position at the Star Tribune was eliminated, ending three decades in newspapering. I started building the next day.

claudewill.io is a platform I built from scratch using AI as my collaborator — HTML, CSS, vanilla JavaScript, Netlify serverless functions, Supabase, Anthropic AI. No frameworks. No templates. Every design decision mine. The code was written in daily conversation with Claude Code, the same way I used to work with designers: here's what I'm thinking, go try it, let's iterate. The method didn't change. The collaborator did.

The centerpiece is CW — a conversational AI modeled after my grandfather, Claude William Simmons, born in Oklahoma Territory in 1903. He worked 51 years. Farmed, worked the railroad, raised 11 children through the Depression and dust bowls. I never met him. So I built a porch where the way he thought about helping people could keep going. You show up with a problem. CW listens, asks questions, gives you a straight answer.

The challenge was building something real without a team, a budget, or a deadline — just the instinct that I don't know how to not make things. The collaborator was a machine, but the process was the same one I'd used for 30 years: talk through the vision, try something, iterate, ship it.

Seventeen months later, the platform is live, I write and build daily, and the practice serves a consulting client in Washington state. The posting asks for HTML, CSS, and JavaScript proficiency. I have a live platform. But the real proof is simpler: the same process that produced a Pulitzer finalist and a Prince commemorative section also produced this. The instinct doesn't retire.

What else

What doesn't fit in projects and resumes

I understand what keeps a newsroom running. I generated $20 million in revenue at the Star Tribune during a pandemic — two paid content verticals, a merchandise program that funded journalism directly. ProPublica is a nonprofit that operates primarily on donations. At a nonprofit, a visual editor who understands institutional sustainability isn't a luxury. It's a practical asset.

I also have an international network of illustrators and photographers I've built over 30 years — artists I can call on a Friday and have original work by Wednesday. That network is the only reason two commissioned paintings were possible in less than a week for the Prince commemorative section. Relationships like that don't appear on a resume, but they're the infrastructure behind the work.

My hope for this position is simple. I've been a lot of things — CCO, VP, executive director, consultant, builder. The work I'm proudest of is the visual journalism. It's what I trained for, what I'm best at, and what I want to do. This role would be a return to that work at a place that values it the way I do.
