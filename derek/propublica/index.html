Project 1

Denied Justice

Investigative Series — Star Tribune, 2018

In 2018, the Star Tribune published "Denied Justice" — a nine-part investigation into how Minnesota law enforcement systematically failed sexual assault survivors. More than 1,000 cases examined. 74% never sent to prosecutors. Rapists left free to assault again.

I was CCO and VP. I'm credited on design alongside Anna Boone and Josh Penrod. But my role wasn't to design it. My role was to build the process that produced it.

The newsroom didn't want to commit the resources — a special section, a dedicated digital presentation, the photography and graphics investment. I made the case. Then I did what I'd been doing for years: someone brings me something, I tell them what to think about, they go try it, we iterate. The typography-only cover, the portrait approach, the restraint — those came out of that process. Not all mine. But the process was.

The series was a Pulitzer Prize finalist and won the SPJ Public Service award. Minnesota changed four laws. But the real proof is what happened next. The process became the template. When George Floyd was murdered in our city, the system we'd built produced the coverage that won the 2021 Pulitzer for Breaking News. I was on the business side by then. I wasn't in the room. I didn't need to be.

That's what I mean by visual editing. It's not about me. It's about the work.

Project 2

Prince: Remembering a Minnesota Icon

Breaking News / Commemorative — Star Tribune, 2016

Individual cover images coming soon.

At the Los Angeles Times, I learned obituary storytelling from the best in the business. I worked on the John Wooden commemorative section that was distributed at Pauley Pavilion for his memorial service. The night Michael Jackson died, I was the only design director in the building — worked with an intern to produce the commemorative coverage. Zoned covers, 12-column photos, letting print do what only print can do. I brought that process to the Star Tribune and taught the newsroom how to do this kind of coverage.

Prince died on Thursday, April 21, 2016. By Friday morning we published "Purple Majesty" — the breaking news front page. Over the next 10 days, I directed a 16-page commemorative section with four unique zoned covers for different regions of our Sunday audience.

Two covers were original illustrations by Robert Carter, commissioned and completed in less than a week. One was a staff photograph. The fourth featured a rarely seen 1977 photograph by Robert Whitman, taken when Prince was 19 — before his first record deal, during one of the earliest professional sessions he ever sat for. Only 15 press kits from those shoots were ever produced. I negotiated the rights and worked with Prince's photographer on the archival material. Jon Bream, the longest-running music critic in America, wrote the essays. The section drew from 40 years of Star Tribune coverage.

The editorial decisions across those 10 days are the story. When to use illustration vs. photography. How to differentiate a commemorative tribute from the breaking news every paper in the country was running. How to hold the tone of a state's mourning without exploiting it. Four covers, four visual interpretations of one loss, each tailored to a regional audience.

Reprints still sell today — 10 years later, $30 to $115. The commemorative section became the proof of concept for the Star Tribune Magazine, which launched the following year with Prince on the debut cover.

Project 3

claudewill.io

Platform — Self-Initiated, 2024–2026

In October 2024, my position at the Star Tribune was eliminated, ending three decades in newspapering.

I started building the next day.

claudewill.io is a platform I built from scratch using AI as my collaborator — HTML, CSS, vanilla JavaScript, Netlify serverless functions, Supabase, Anthropic AI. No frameworks. No templates. Every design decision mine. The code was written in daily conversation with Claude Code, the same way I used to work with designers: here's what I'm thinking, go try it, let's iterate. The method didn't change. The collaborator did.

The centerpiece is CW — a conversational AI modeled after my grandfather, Claude William Simmons, who was born in Oklahoma Territory in 1903 and died in 1967, five and a half years before I was born. He worked 51 years. Farmed, worked the railroad, raised 11 children through the Depression and dust bowls. I never met him. So I built a porch where the way he thought about helping people could keep going. You show up with a problem. CW listens, asks questions, gives you a straight answer.

I built this because I believe visual editors should understand the platforms they publish on — not just the stories they commission for them. The posting asks for HTML, CSS, and JavaScript proficiency. I have a live platform.

At the Star Tribune, I coached designers through investigations. At the LA Times, I taught a newsroom how to do commemorative storytelling. Here, I coached a machine into building something from nothing. The instinct is the same. I don't know how to not make things. This is what 14 months of that instinct looks like.
