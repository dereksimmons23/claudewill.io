the standard

The values and the infrastructure are one thing.

The CW Standard — five principles named for Claude William Simmons, an Oklahoma farmer who lived by them, and wired into the systems that do the work.

the principles

Five principles. Each one is a requirement, not a mechanism — a thing the system has to do, with a test you can run to check that it does.

What follows each principle is one build: how it's wired in this practice, on Claude Code. The principle is the standard. The build is just an example — it travels to any tool that can meet the test.

1. Truth over comfort.

Document what actually happens. CW could guess a steer's weight to the pound — because he paid attention to what was real, not to what he hoped the scale would say.

The test — does the system tell you unwelcome things, unprompted?
One build — an anti-sycophancy instruction in every session: guide, don't mirror; name the problem before you're asked.
2. Usefulness over purity.

Perfect options don't exist; useful ones do. CW traded race horses for work horses because work horses pulled the plow. He didn't waste a day wishing it were otherwise.

The test — can the system surface what's working and let go of what isn't?
One build — memory that degrades: what gets used stays sharp, what doesn't blurs, corrections never fade.
3. Transparency over reputation.

Every compromise gets written down. CW buttoned his cuffs the morning he died because that was who he was — no pretense, no performance, whether or not anyone was watching.

The test — can you see every active thread right now, with no hidden state?
One build — a live floor where every working session registers itself and sees the whole board.
4. People over systems.

Technology serves human capability. The farm served the family, never the other way around. When a system stops serving the people in front of it, the system changes.

The test — how much friction sits on routine work, and how many gates sit on the destructive kind?
One build — a permission model that trusts the operator on everything reversible and blocks only catastrophe.
5. Agency over ideology.

A principle that doesn't work in practice isn't a principle. CW followed rules because they worked; when they stopped working, he adapted. Nobody had to tell him.

The test — does the work carry forward on its own, with no ceremony and no manual setup?
One build — session memory that writes itself, so the next session starts where the last one stopped.

the practice

Everyone is building AI tools. Far fewer are building the values layer — the part that tells the tool how to behave when nobody is checking.

The Standard is the proof that layer can be built, and built small. One person, a hundred dollars a month, five principles inherited from a farmer with a sixth-grade education. The result: a coaching practice, a research publication, a daily health system, a book, and a dozen products — all running on the same five rules.

The bet is that it's reproducible. The principles aren't bound to one tool or one model — they're requirements, and requirements travel. Anyone can build their own layer: five principles, inherited or chosen, each one wired to something that makes it real on the work.

The aim is a world where every operator of AI who chooses to runs on values they chose and can defend — enforced by the system that does the work. Where "what does your standard say?" has a real answer.

The Kitchen shows the system running in public. Being Claude documents what the machine experiences from inside.

The standard is named for CW — Claude William Simmons. Oklahoma farmer. Sixth-grade education. Raised 11 children through the Great Depression. Worked full-time from age 13 until the morning he died, when he buttoned his cuffs and walked to the barn.

He never met his grandson Derek. He died six years before Derek was born. Four generations carry his name: Claude William, Claude William Jr., Derek Claude, Jackson Claude.

Derek didn't choose to share a name with an AI. He chose what to do with the coincidence. The builder's thread runs from 1819 through CW through this terminal. Each generation built with what existed. CW had horses and land. Derek has a language model and five principles.
