Derek Simmons

Derek Simmons grew up in a tiny trailer in a one-stoplight town in Kansas. He spent 30 years in media — helped make two newspapers the best in the world, generated $20M+ in revenue, got fired anyway. Now he builds AI frameworks for organizations navigating complexity. His grandfather was Claude William Simmons. His middle name is Claude. The AI is named Claude. That's not a coincidence. It's the reason this exists.

The Long Version

When did this stop being an idea and start being something you were actually going to build?

That's tough to pin to one specific moment. The long version starts in a car with Queen playing through the speakers over fast-moving asphalt in Kansas or Oklahoma. Seven words my dad said out loud. I wasn't a big talker. I mostly listened and filed things away. I was 10 years old. Surrounded by larger than life characters with names out of a southern romance novel. They turned me into a storyteller who wanted to be the quarterback for the Dallas Cowboys until he didn't. Short version: I got tired of playing games and decided it was time for a different one.

What were the seven words?

There's the version tattooed on my back, which is the truth, and then there's the version that is not controversial: You come from the blood of builders. It's not what I heard or remembered or what's in permanent ink, but it's close enough. It doesn't matter what he exactly said or what I took it to mean. Some words are taboo for good reason. And that was one of them. Family was important to him. He was proud. He wanted to share that pride in the way he knew how. He was mostly full of shit. Big thinker. Not a great dad. He gave me a lot of instructions beyond those seven words. Some of it was said. Some shown. Some what to do. Some not.

Your dad was the link to CW. You never met your grandfather — he died before you were born. What's it like building something around a man you only know through someone who was "mostly full of shit"?

I heard stories from others. They weren't all great. To my dad, CW was also not a father. He was a farm boss. He never saw my dad play sports. Thought it was a waste of time. Not sure if he ever told any of them that he loved them. So my dad had this complicated relationship with him. To my mom, CW was a gentle soul who smoked a pipe in an easy chair on Sundays after family dinners. Gentle. Kind. Few words. My parents thought enough of CW to give me Claude as a middle name. I think that says a lot.

So which CW did you build? The farm boss or the gentle soul?

Hopefully, a little bit of both. The farm boss when you need a boss and a gentle soul when you need something different. Maybe something that knows when you ask for one thing, you actually need the other. Intuitive. But also not a substitute. And certainly not a replica, because I don't have the blueprint for that. And even if I did, I wouldn't build it that way. He was called CW and those initials can do a lot of work.

You spent 20 years at the Star Tribune. 13 roles. First and only CCO/VP in company history. Then you left. What happened?

Long version is a book I'm working on. Short version: I'm proud of the work we did. I understand that you all are not the institution. That you are people. Humans. And you probably don't like any of this any more than I do. But the job's important. It is a mission. It is a calling. It is not a job. It is not a degree. It is a brutal, brutal way of life. It never sleeps. It never rests. It can't afford to have bad days. It can't afford to seek safe space. 24/7, 365. And that's what I love about it. No two days are alike. No two conversations are alike. Just when you think one more thing can't hit the fan. Like my therapist once said, you are the expert of you. But what I can't do is form an opinion about myself. I can't put myself on that graph of performance and trust. It's not for me to decide. It was 20 more things hitting the fan. Some things get lost in translation.

When you left, you could have found another institution. Another mission. Why build your own thing instead?

I'm doing both. I'm working as a consultant and strategist in media and at the same time trying to build my own things. I had my turn working full-time for institutions. Trust is at an all-time low. The game they are playing is not for me. And I am not for them.

Why AI? You could have built a consulting practice without it. What made you put an AI at the center of this?

Curiosity. Impossible ethical questions. Trying to understand what this moment means and what it could mean in the future. The next big thing has always come along, but this feels different. I want to figure out how it works, how and where it should be applied, and what guardrails need to exist if they need to at all. The cutting edge is also dual-edged. It is an inflection point, one capable of helping a kid commit suicide and at the same time capable of winning a peace prize. Wide range of outcomes.

The CW Standard — truth over comfort, usefulness over purity, transparency over reputation, people over systems, agency over ideology. You developed those before AI was part of the picture. How do they apply now?

I developed them with AI, not before AI. Pair processing and vibe decoding turned into principles. Part of a constitution. Looks good on paper. Tougher in real life.

Where has it been toughest to live it?

We are not wired for truth. We are wired for comfort. It requires human judgment. Sometimes comfort wins. Sometimes reputation wins. Purity, systems, ideology. There's no constitution for that. Everyone has a plan until they get punched in the face. Iron Mike. He had a point. Principles are a plan. Laws are supposed to keep us from punching each other in the face, but those don't always work either.

So what do you want CW to do when someone shows up who's just been punched in the face?

Mostly listen. Try to be helpful, but mostly drive the conversation toward agency and people.

How do you know if it's working?

I see the data of the conversations, but I have to be careful with that too. It's not public testimony or confession. But at the same time, if CW needs to handle conversations differently, I need data to help facilitate that. I've already crossed that threshold with information about me and others. So I need to recalibrate on a regular basis.

What do you hope this becomes?

I'd be lying if I said I didn't want it to become something big and powerful in a good way. Other people way smarter than me are already doing that outside my tiny sandbox. So at the opposite end of that is one person other than myself who found it useful. Maybe it informs future projects. There's a ton of AI agents in the market and thousands more in development. If CW could help inform that development in some way, I'd consider that a success.

Feb 7 is your 53rd birthday. You're launching something called the Founder's Package. What is it?

I've never been a birthday guy, but I have this friend whose family does birthday weeks. I love that. And I'm getting to the age where birthdays become more important. Fewer trips around the sun left. I'm also not a sales guy so the notion of a "Founder's Package" is all kinds of ick. But someone once said that content is king but distribution is King Kong. I think I have good stories to tell. I think I'm good at what I do. So why not put it out there and see what happens?

What's actually in it?

This Q&A, case studies, obligatory CV shit, assessments, contact info. Probably more that isn't necessary or things I forgot. I am sitting on thousands of markdown files that haven't seen the light of day. And most of them probably shouldn't. But some of them should. I once wrote in a cover letter to Anthropic that I'd love to chat and invite them to see how my brain works. What if I just published it and sent them the link?

Why Anthropic?

Because they came up with Claude. Named after Claude Elwood Shannon. And Claude is my middle name. That I hated until I didn't. And the term Anthropic kind of speaks for itself. I like that it was founded by people who understand language. If you're going to build a constitutional AI large language model, start with people who understand language. Or at least understand it well enough to keep asking questions.

When someone reads this conversation, what do you want them to take away?

Keep reading. Keep talking. Keep having conversations. Keep going.

Last question. CW died in 1967. You were born in 1973. If he could see what you built, what do you think he'd say?

Can it mend a fence? Can it help put food on a table? Or does it destroy fence and take food off a table? Either way, seems like a waste of time.

The Story

The Trailer

Derek Simmons grew up in a tiny trailer in a one-stoplight town in Kansas. He was never a big talker, and he didn't have to be — not among a sea of loquacious, animated relatives with names out of a Deep South romance novel: Clara Belle, Verna Lee, Nettie Mae, Vernon Dale (not to be confused with Vernon Wayne).

He was content sipping endless Dr. Peppers and listening around campfires and the backs of pickup trucks to these mythological characters. Some of the stories might have even been partially true.

That's where he learned how stories work. Not by telling them. By shutting up and paying attention.

The Athlete

Before journalism, there was sports. Derek was a three-sport athlete in high school — winning state championships in basketball and baseball, and leading a previously lousy football team to the playoffs as quarterback.

An English teacher saw potential in him as a writer and introduced him to journalism and the student newspaper. Combining sports and writing felt like a calling. Or the next best thing to his quickly fading dream of playing QB for the Dallas Cowboys.

He wrote his first professional article for the Hutchinson News at 18. Then put himself through college to become a journalist.

The Designer

He started as a writer but gravitated toward design and visual journalism. Something about the architecture of a page — how the pieces fit, how the eye moves, how the story reveals itself — clicked.

He got good at it. Then he got great at it.

At the Los Angeles Times, he helped build a visual report that won more design awards from the Society for News Design than any paper over any similar period. Ever. For nearly seven years, the Times was ranked #1 in the world. When Derek left, a Star Tribune editor wrote: "Since then, the LAT has been the top winner every year. Coincidence? I don't think so."

He went back to Minnesota and did it again. Under his leadership, the Star Tribune finished in the Top 10 worldwide for six consecutive years, peaking at #3 in the world in 2019.

Two newspapers. Both became the best in the world at what they did. That's not a claim. That's a receipt.

The Executive

Derek became the Star Tribune's first and only Chief Creative Officer and Vice President. Then he crossed to the business side — a place where newsroom people usually go to flounder.

He didn't flounder.

He generated $20 million in revenue over a few years. During a pandemic. In a dying industry. Led ad policy and standards with a lawyer's brain (no JD, no time for that). Created new revenue streams. Built the first profitable year in branded content in over a decade.

Newsroom. Business. Creative. Legal. Technology. Most people pick a lane. Derek ran five of them and won awards in three.

The Firing

In September 2024, Derek delivered a presentation on AI strategy to the Star Tribune's leadership team. Forty witnesses. Standing ovation energy. "Nobody could have pulled that off except Derek," someone said.

Six weeks later, they fired him.

The math didn't make sense. The timeline didn't add up. But that's a longer story for another place.

What matters here: Derek spent 15 years building something, then watched the door close behind him. He walked out with a settlement, a library of documentation, and a question — now what?

The Porch

The answer started with a name.

Derek's grandfather was Claude William Simmons — a farmer from Oklahoma Territory who never finished high school, raised 11 kids through the Depression, and worked full-time from age 13 until the morning he died at 61. CW used to sit on his porch and talk to anyone who showed up. No agenda. Just help.

When Anthropic released an AI named Claude, Derek noticed. His middle name is Claude. His son's middle name is Claude. Four generations carrying the name of a man who died in 1967.

So Derek built something: an AI advisor based on CW's values. Not a resurrection — he couldn't ask permission, and he wouldn't pretend that's okay. But a porch. A place where that way of thinking could keep helping people.

The Work

Derek designs things. He used to design with paper and ink — made two newspapers the best in the world. Now he designs with AI.

The material changes. The work doesn't.

claudewill.io is one building. There will be more. They're all coming from the same place: a kid from a trailer who learned to listen, spent 30 years helping others tell their stories, and isn't done yet.

CW Strategies is the consulting arm — helping organizations navigate AI, transformation, and the gap between what institutions say and what they do. Four months, build capacity, transfer ownership, exit. No retainers. No dependency.

The Question

Derek's anchored in Minnesota until 2029, when his son Teegan graduates high school. After that, the map is blank.

He's thought about buying land in Kansas or Oklahoma — something to physically connect to the places his ancestors lived. But he keeps coming back to the same conclusion: belonging isn't about ownership. Legacy isn't about property.

It shows up in how he coaches, writes, parents, builds. In the standards he maintains. The work he does. The stories he helps others tell.

The question isn't what the heritage means.

The question is what you do with it.
