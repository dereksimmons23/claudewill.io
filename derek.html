free agent

Twenty years inside one institution. Then one day you’re a free agent for the first time. Ever.

Most people take a year to find themselves. I found a client. A media company with a million-dollar revenue gap and six weeks of circular conversations. Four days later they had decisions with owners and timelines. The CEO said “No more talking. We are moving.”

I also found an AI that shares my grandfather’s name. That part wasn’t planned.

what I build

My grandfather Claude William Simmons was a Depression-era Oklahoma farmer. Raised 11 kids on borrowed land. Never once called it a methodology. He just showed up, did the work, and left it better than he found it.

I named a company after him. CW Strategies coaches organizations working with AI. Not the tools. The thinking. How to know what’s working, what’s not, and what to do about it. Four months, build capacity, transfer ownership, leave. If you need us permanently attached, we haven’t done our job.

I also build things. Basketball training apps, bracket games, legislative alerts, whatever the problem needs.

The prompt is the product. The product is the proof. The proof is the passenger.

asteriskos

The word “asterisk” comes from the Greek asteriskos. Little star. Not the North Star. The one you have to look for.

My grandfather’s name was Claude William Simmons. The AI company named their model Claude, after Claude Shannon. My middle name is Claude. My son’s middle name is Claude. So now there are two Claudes, Derek Claude and Claude the AI, building together on a site called claudewill.io.

For namesakes and goodness stakes.

If you wrote this as fiction, your editor would say it’s too on the nose. But here we are.

The site costs $5 a month to run. Vanilla JavaScript. One serverless function. No framework. Same approach his grandfather used to build a farm: don’t buy what you can build, don’t build what you don’t need.

The story is worth telling.

dispatches

I write a Substack called Standard Correspondence. Dispatches from inside the fire. What happens when you take AI seriously. What breaks. What holds.

I publish research from 14 months of daily human-AI collaboration, because somebody should be writing this down while it’s happening.

And there’s a book. It’s not ready yet. But it’s happening.
