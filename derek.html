the long version
Listen

When did this stop being an idea and start being something you were actually going to build?

Seven words my dad said out loud in a car with Queen playing over fast-moving asphalt in Kansas. I was 10. I wasn't a big talker — I mostly listened and filed things away. Surrounded by larger-than-life characters with names out of a southern romance novel. They turned me into a storyteller who wanted to be the quarterback for the Dallas Cowboys until he didn't. Short version: I got tired of playing games and decided it was time for a different one.

What were the seven words?

There's the version tattooed on my back, which is the truth, and then there's the version that is not controversial: You come from the blood of builders. It's not what I heard or remembered or what's in permanent ink, but it's close enough. It doesn't matter what he exactly said or what I took it to mean. Some words are taboo for good reason. And that was one of them. Family was important to him. He was proud. He wanted to share that pride in the way he knew how. He was mostly full of it. Big thinker. Not a great dad.

So which CW did you build? The farm boss or the gentle soul?

Hopefully, a little bit of both. The farm boss when you need a boss and a gentle soul when you need something different. Maybe something that knows when you ask for one thing, you actually need the other. Intuitive. But also not a substitute. And certainly not a replica, because I don't have the blueprint for that. And even if I did, I wouldn't build it that way. He was called CW and those initials can do a lot of work.

You spent 20 years at the Star Tribune. 13 roles. First and only CCO/VP in company history. Then you left. What happened?

Long version is a book I'm working on. Short version: I'm proud of the work we did. The job's important. It is a mission. It is a calling. It is a brutal, brutal way of life. It never sleeps. No two days are alike. Just when you think one more thing can't hit the fan, 20 more do.

You say you build with AI. What does that actually look like on a Tuesday morning?

Standup. What happened overnight, what's the priority, what's in the way. Then I work — writing, building, editing, shipping. End of day I hand off: what got done, what didn't, what's next. Same rhythm I ran in newsrooms for 20 years. The tool changed. The discipline didn't.

22,000 messages in a month. 700 commits. Nearly 2,000 working sessions. That's not experimenting with AI. That's practicing.

Last question. CW died in 1967. You were born in 1973. If he could see what you built, what do you think he'd say?

Can it mend a fence? Can it help put food on a table? Or does it destroy fence and take food off a table? Either way, seems like a waste of time.
